FOSSILS

26 September 2024·14 mins

Introduction #

The modern world stands on the foundation of materials discovered in recent decades, highlighting the impact of scientific discovery and technological advances [@blessing_advancements_2023]. At the heart of this transformative landscape is the interdisciplinary field of materials science, where innovation is driven by the collaborative efforts of researchers from diverse academic backgrounds, ranging from molecular chemistry to applied machine learning [@malek_virtual_2019].

However, within this collaborative framework, certain bottlenecks hinder the pace of discovery. The ability to rapidly iterate through the scientific discovery process is important for addressing urgent challenges, as seen in responses to the COVID-19 pandemic and the ongoing climate crisis [@pyzer-knapp_accelerating_2022]. In addition, the anticipation of new challenges, such as energy scarcity, underscores the growing need to accelerate scientific discovery [@coyle_understanding_2014]. This requires removing collaborative barriers, automating repetitive tasks, and establishing seamless communication and standards among researchers.

{#fig:labinthelop width="\linewidth"}

Primary bottlenecks in sciences requiring experimentation are the time and resource constraints. In healthcare, for example, the development of a drug typically requires a decade or more of effort and significant financial investment in excess of $2 billion [@das_accelerated_2021]. In materials science, bottlenecks extend beyond testing molecular properties to the time-consuming process of creating and combining new substances. The vast space of potential materials requires strategic approaches to prioritize candidate molecules for synthesis. Traditional ‘random walk’ searches are ineffective, prompting the use of active search techniques and recommendation systems [@lookman_active_2019].

In response to this, there are works attempting to solve this issue and automate the entire process. For example, @das_accelerated_2021 uses Wasserstein Auto-Encoders (WAE) to model peptide latent space and generate potential candidates without manual feature extraction, leveraging both labeled and unlabeled AMP sequences. However, given our limited labeled dataset (34 distinct molecules with SMILES), deep learning methods are not suitable, especially considering their data-hungry nature and our requirement for symmetric SMILES. Conversely, @pyzer-knapp_accelerating_2022 propose an automated discovery workflow, which our approaches can integrate seamlessly, particularly in data extraction and new thesis proposal stages where we generate and rank new molecules based on predicted capacity. Additionally, @zhu_artificial_2018 and @saad_data-driven_2022 present solutions utilizing machine learning for capacitance prediction using features from lab experiments, which renders their approaches incompatible with our automated workflow. Moreover, the two former works suffer from data leakage due to not separating molecules of the same family during training and testing. Notably, they do not generate new molecules, setting our approach apart. Addressing this limitation, our contributions seek to redefine the problem setting for accelerating battery materials discovery under the example of Covalent Triazine Frameworks (CTFs) [@liao_advances_2023].

In this paper, we introduce a comprehensive end-to-end solution that generates new molecules and predicts the target capacity of CTFs based solely on the encoded representation of the molecular substrates in Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMILES) [@smiles]. Such an approach eliminates the need for expensive and lengthy additional in-lab experimental measurements, required in a typical scientific discovery process. On top of that, we also developed a user interface that aims to facilitate communication between lab scientists and our solution.

Specifically, our contributions include:

  • Introduction of a machine learning pipeline for end-to-end capacitance estimation based solely on SMILES strings. This replaces lengthy experimental feature generation with computational feature engineering, accelerating the discovery process.

  • Development of a computational toolkit for estimating structural properties in Porous Carbon Materials (PCMs). This toolkit comprises algorithms for calculating molecular symmetry, estimating molecule geometric flatness, determining pore size, and estimating pore volume using the PoreBlazer [@sarkisov_poreblazer] library. Packaged as a separate Python module1, it facilitates potential integrations with other frameworks.

  • Proposal of a procedural generation method for symmetric molecules, aimed at saving computational time during candidate screening. This approach contrasts recent trends in molecule generation utilizing deep learning [@das_accelerated_2021; @das_pepcvae_2018]. However, due to data scarcity, deep learning approaches are impractical for our problem setting. Generating symmetric molecules immediately requires significantly more symmetric data in the dataset, far beyond what we currently possess.

  • In addition to the technical framework outlined, we developed a web app for the lab-in-the-loop approach that integrates three key features tailored to molecular chemists: seamless importation of new molecules, a user-friendly editor for swift discovery, and a ranking visualization interface. These functionalities streamline the exploration process, allowing chemists to efficiently assess and manipulate molecular data.

  • Collection and exploration of datasets containing previously researched Porous Carbon Materials.

Methods #

Data collection #

We obtained a dataset from our collaborators consisting of 34 distinct molecules gathered from previously published papers on CTFs. However, the total count reached 65 examples due to some molecules being duplicated, albeit synthesized under different conditions (such as temperature or solvent), resulting in different measured capacities. Furthermore, we explored additional datasets in the literature and identified two containing relevant PCMs from supercapacitors. One dataset, as reported by @zhu_artificial_2018, comprises 681 molecules, while the second dataset, documented by @saad_data-driven_2022, contains 925 molecules.

We subsequently chose to analyze these two datasets separately: one termed "single dataset," comprising solely the data received from our experts, and the other labeled "combined dataset," which was created by aggregating all available data.

Data cleaning #

First, we rename and align columns with the same features from different datasets. Then, we construct three different targets: (1) mean H2SO4 capacity from CV and GCD measurements, (2) mean NaOH capacity from CV and GCD measurements, and (3) total mean capacity, which is the average value of (1) and (2). Next, we assign groups to chemical molecules based on their SMILES to establish a clear validation scheme that ensures no data leakage for experiments.

For each prediction task, we remove molecules that have missing target values. Typically, there are H2SO4 capacity measurements available, but NaOH measurements are missing, and vice versa. We filter out these cases.

Feature enigneering #

In situations where access to high-quality data is restricted, the importance of feature engineering becomes crucial. It is essential to obtain informative and useful features to enhance the overall analysis. Consequently, we integrate several approaches to extract valuable features solely from the SMILES string associated with a given molecule.

Molecular symmetry #

The information regarding the geometric spatial symmetry of a molecule holds significance in understanding its potential for CTFs. According to @liao_advances_2023, molecules exhibiting high symmetry are more promising to form proper rings and subsequent frameworks. Consequently, our investigation focuses on extracting the standalone feature of the symmetry group of molecules.

To achieve this objective, we employed the Pymatgen [@pymatgen] library, enabling the calculation of point groups for molecules. A point group, defined as a mathematical group of symmetry operations sharing a common fixed point, implies that objects in the same group, when subjected to identical geometric transformations (e.g., rotation around the center, rotation around some plane), maintain symmetry. Molecules are assigned to predefined symmetry groups, each associated with specific symmetry operations. For instance, the $C_s$ group signifies molecules symmetric to their mirror plane image, while the $C_i$ group indicates molecules preserving symmetry after reflection through some inversion center. For a detailed characterization and introduction to molecular symmetry using point groups refer to @physicalChemistry.

For the purposes of this paper, we treat these symmetries as categorical features. Notably, the $C_1$ group represents molecules devoid of any symmetry. From the information on molecular symmetries, we derive two features: "categorical_symmetry" and "binary_symmetry." The former represents the group symbol treated as a categorical variable, while the latter serves as a binary variable indicating the presence or absence of any symmetry.

Molecular flatness #

It is argued that in order to form CTFs the molecules must comprise a foiled (i.e., flat) core. Therefore, we introduce a simple approach aiming to capture the degree to which the molecule is spatially flat. We present a visualization of this approach in 2{reference-type=“ref+Label” reference=“fig:flatness”}.

To determine molecular flatness, we utilize a straightforward approach involving fitting of a linear regression (LR) model to the three-dimensional embedding of the examined molecule. Subsequently, we calculate the standard error, representing the average distance of atoms from the plane. To derive the coordinates of each atom in the molecule, we employ the generation of so-called "conformers" using the RDKit library [@rdkit]. Conformers serve as spatial embeddings, providing a set of coordinates for the molecule. It is important to note that a molecule may possess multiple correct spatial embeddings due to its inherent flexibility.

In order to approximate the expected flatness of the molecule, we compute coordinates for a total of $k$ distinct conformers. For each conformer, we independently apply a LR model. Subsequently, standard errors are determined by calculating root mean square errors (RMSE) for each LR model, and these individual errors are averaged to obtain a comprehensive measure of flatness for the molecule.

Pore size estimation #

Pore size is a property of a porous material that is correlated with the target capacity [@su_predicting_2019]. The possibility of estimating it solely from the base molecule allows for an extension of accessible datasets. We implemented an algorithm for pore size estimation that assumes a hexagonal ring structure of a CTF base unit. Integration with PoreBlazer #

The integration of PoreBlazer [@sarkisov_poreblazer] into our computational methodology allowed us to further increase the number of features. PoreBlazer is a molecular simulation software designed to characterize and quantify pore size distributions, accessible surface areas, and other critical parameters of porous materials, utilizing algorithms based on geometric and energetic considerations.

Automated Workflow #

The proposed workflow is enclosed in an end-to-end automated pipeline capable of generating new molecules, estimating their target capacity, and ranking them accordingly. The entire process is designed to facilitate a lab-in-the-loop approach, enabling experts to utilize estimated capacity information of molecules. This allows them to focus experimental synthesis efforts only on those molecules that show potential for high capacity. We depict the proposed system facilitating the mentioned approach in 1{reference-type=“ref+Label” reference=“fig:labinthelop”}.

Molecule Generation #

The number of molecules documented in the literature is limited. In this project, our goal is to identify novel molecules that could serve as plausible building blocks for CTFs. To achieve this, we propose a proof-of-concept molecule generation method capable of producing these new molecules.

First, we utilize the TSV2 library [@tsv2] to generate a set of candidate molecules from a specified scaffold molecule. The TSV2 is a library equipped with a pre-trained model designed for generating new molecules based on a scaffold. We leverage this library to expand the molecule by attaching new atoms and forming additional bonds, extending in only one spatial dimension.

Then, we enhance the symmetry of the generated candidates by converting them into graphs, identifying their core, and duplicating the grown branch on the opposite side of the core. This simple yet effective heuristic ensures symmetry, particularly for supported types of molecules, such as those featuring a benzene core. An illustration of this pipeline is provided in 4{reference-type=“ref+Label” reference=“fig:generation”}, demonstrating the process starting from a single benzene core, through molecule generation using TSV2, and concluding with symmetrization.

Feature and target selection #

Our interest lies in developing models that can effectively rank these molecules based on their potential energy capacity, from highest to lowest. To achieve this, we train models specifically to predict the energy capacity of each molecule, and then we arrange them in order based on these predicted values.

Molecule ranking #

We train three common machine learning algorithms: LightGBM, selected from Microsoft’s LightGBM package [@ke2017lightgbm], and RF and KNN implemented from the popular scikit-learn library [@scikit-learn].

To guarantee a thorough evaluation of each model’s performance, we employed Grouped Cross Validation using 10 Folds. This approach grouped the molecules according to their SMILES encoding, which helped prevent any data leakage during the evaluation phase. Additionally, we assessed the accuracy of the predicted capacity by measuring the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE).

In the experiments, we use the following hyperparameters for the models:

  • LigthGBM Regressor

    • max_depth := 3

    • boosting_type := goss

    • learning_rate := 0.03

    • n_estimators := 250

    • min_data_in_leaf := 2

    • min_data_in_bin := 1

    • colsample_bytree := 0.6

  • RandomForest Regressor

    • max_depth := 3

    • n_estimators := 250

  • K-Nearest Neighbors Regressor with "K" set to 3

Application #

A crucial step in our lab-in-the-loop approach is the implementation of a web application specifically targeting the needs of molecular chemists. To achieve this goal, we decided to include three key features in our system: the ability to import arbitrary SMILES, an editor for quick discovery, and a ranking visualization. Exemplary screenshots of the application interface are provided in 5{reference-type=“ref+Label” reference=“fig:app”}.

In order to develop our application, we have made the following technical decisions:

  • We decided to use client-side technologies, in particular React.js. Our goal was to ensure full interactivity and immediate responses. We also extended our solution with React Router to utilize client-side routing, resulting in short fetching and loading times.

  • For the purposes of building an attractive, user-friendly, and accessible application, we turned to the MUI library, which gave us access to dozens of pre-created components, available for quick integration into our React components.

  • To build our molecule editor, we decided to turn to an off-the-shelf solution, the Ketcher library [@ketcher]. We utilize its client-side parsing library, paired with easy-to-integrate React components.

  • The molecule ranking is displayed in our application in the form of a card grid, allowing for effective exploration and filtering. Additionally, each card is paired with a visualization of its SMILES representation, created using the SmilesDrawer JavaScript library [@smiles-drawer], which can be manually edited and exported to CSV format with SMILES representation and ranking position.

  • We built the backend of our application using Python and FastAPI library, allowing for seamless integration with our machine learning models and other developed components.

  • The frontend of our application was deployed in the Azure Cloud, using a Static Web App solution. This allows for easy online access to our resources by chemists and other interested parties.

Results #

The main goal of experiments is to create a proper ranking of candidate molecules based on their potential to form high-capacitance CTFs. To achieve this, we design an experiment of pairwise ranking, which in essence is comparing all possible pairs of molecules and deciding on which of them has higher capacity, without explicitly calculating this value. Such an approach to the problem allows for turning the regression problem of estimating the target capacity numerically into a binary classification problem performed in pairs of molecules. The baseline for this experiment is randomly selecting the winning molecule, which results in 50% correct pairwise comparisons (i.e., comparison accuracy).

Single data set #

We used features that could be potentially extracted from SMILES and were available in all data sets, namely:

  • Flatness

  • Symmetry

  • Pore Size

  • Pore Volume

All the models we trained in the experiments were unable to beat the baseline and on average, performed on par with it, achieving the same ranking accuracy.

Combined data set #

On the combined dataset, we solely utilize pore size and pore volume features to estimate the target capacity. This limitation arises from the lack of access to SMILES encodings for these molecules, with only pore size and pore volume experimental values reported. In the pairwise ranking experiment, the top-performing model, LightGBM, achieves a pairwise ranking accuracy of 74%. Intuitively, this indicates that 74% of pairwise comparisons resulted in a correct ranking.

Discussion #

Our experiments indicate that the proposed approach fails to achieve the desired goal with our experts’ dataset. Despite various attempts, all experiments resulted in models being unable to outperform the simple baseline approach. We attribute this poor performance to the small number of training examples available. Our experimental setup, utilizing Group K-Fold, ensures that molecules of the same scaffold cannot be used in both training and testing datasets. Consequently, we end up with only a few groups, making the problem exceedingly challenging for any machine learning tool due to insufficient data. We propose that this issue could be addressed by expanding the dataset through the extraction of SMILES strings from the two datasets used for the "combined dataset" variation. These datasets provide the measured capacity and link each molecule to its respective publication, facilitating the possible extraction of SMILES strings.

The above hypothesis gains further support from experiments conducted with datasets obtained from two other works, which demonstrate that the features selected for our model are informative and capable of generating a satisfactory ranking of molecules. This outcome underscores the importance of enhancing our experts’ CTF dataset as the next crucial step. Doing so will likely lead to the development of a model that surpasses the baseline and performs satisfactorily.
